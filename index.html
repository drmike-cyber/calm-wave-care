Parent’s guide

Your child’s “rest-and-digest” system, in plain language

The vagus nerve is the longest cranial nerve in the body, linking the brainstem to the heart, lungs, stomach, and intestines. It’s the main pathway of the parasympathetic nervous system — the “rest-and-digest” side that helps the body slow down, digest, and feel safe.

A helpful way to picture the nervous system is three gears it shifts between through the day:

  • Calm & connected — the settled, rest-and-digest state where children sleep, digest, focus, and engage with the people around them.
  • Fight-or-flight — the revved-up, alert state the body uses to meet a challenge.
  • Shutdown — the withdrawn, “checked-out” state a system drops into when it’s overwhelmed.

When a child can move back toward that first state more easily, daily life tends to feel better — calmer mornings, easier digestion, steadier sleep. Gentle, hands-on care is one way to support the body in finding that calmer gear.

A straight word: this is a way of understanding the nervous system that many clinicians find genuinely useful — a lens for care, and one the science is still actively discussing. We link both sides in our Resources, because we’d rather you read it yourself.

Everyday foundations

Small habits that help kids feel their best

Alongside hands-on care, these are the simple, whole-family foundations Dr. Mike helps parents build on — the everyday basics that make the biggest difference in how a child feels.

Steady, restful sleep

A predictable wind-down and a calm, dim sleep space help a child’s nervous system downshift into rest. Consistent bedtimes, less screen light in the final hour, and a soothing routine give the body the cues it needs to settle and stay asleep.

Movement and play

Free, joyful movement is one of the best regulators a child has. Climbing, swinging, rolling, and rough-and-tumble play feed the balance and body-awareness systems that help kids feel organized, capable, and at home in their bodies.

Balanced nourishment

Steady blood sugar makes for steadier moods and focus. Protein-forward meals, real whole foods, and regular snacks keep a child’s energy even — the practical, food-first foundation Dr. Mike’s clinical-nutrition guidance builds on.

A calmer baseline

Children co-regulate with the calm adults around them. Slow breathing, unhurried transitions, and plenty of quiet, connected time help a busy nervous system find its rest-and-digest gear — at home and in the treatment room.

Common questions

Honest answers, plainly given

What techniques do you use?

Gentle chiropractic adjustments, manual and soft-tissue therapy, cranial and craniosacral-style work, polyvagal-informed bodywork, breath and regulation coaching, and clinical-nutrition guidance — chosen and combined for each child’s age, comfort, and needs.

What happens at a first visit?

We start with a relaxed conversation about your child’s history and what you’re hoping for. Care is unhurried and consent-based, you stay beside your child the whole time, and we go only as far as your child is comfortable going that day.

Is this care safe for babies and children?

Yes — Dr. Mike uses gentle, low-force, child-sized techniques, and nothing happens without your understanding and consent. He has a special clinical interest in neurodivergent children and tailors everything to the child in front of him.

Will this treat or cure my child’s autism?

No — and be cautious of anyone who promises that. Dr. Mike doesn’t diagnose, treat, or cure autism or any developmental condition. What he offers is gentle, comfort-focused care and warm support for your child and family, working alongside the medical team already caring for them. Medication and medical decisions always stay with you and your child’s doctors.
